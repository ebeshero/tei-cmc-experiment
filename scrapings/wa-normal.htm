On Jan 30, 2004, at 3:09 PM, Ross Scaife wrote:

> Do members of this list believe that, for legal purposes, implementing
> TEI-XML markup on an e-text that previously lacked it creates
> essentially a new edition?
>
> And likewise, would substantially extending already existing TEI markup
> create a new edition?
>
>

For "legal purposes" the concept of a "new edition" has no meaining.
In the US, at least, the legal question is whether or not the TEI-XML
markup added contains sufficient originality to render the resulting
work copyrightable.  If the markup added does no more than indicate in
an e-text the textual features of a hordcopy edition (e.g., using
elements such as <p>, <head>, <pb>, <hi>, etc.) I would say that the
resulting work contains nothing original.  Anyone doing a competent job
of this kind of text markup would necessarily arrive at the same, or at
least an extremely similar, text.

On the other hand, if the markup added evidences a new reading of the
e-text, it might well result in the creation of a new, copyrightable
work.  The copyright in this case extends only to the added markup, not
to the underlying work, and assumes either that the previously existing
e-text is in the public domain or that the person adding the markup
does so with the permission of the holder of the copyright in the
original e-text.  If neither of these conditions applies then the added
markup is simply a violation of copyright.
